<h1><a id="post-354-_Toc90453895"></a>Introduction</h1>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Humans, especially creative ones, are a visual species. We digest complex and nuanced information by drawing it out. Diagramming out complex ideas can help us see the subtleties, the interconnections between elements and where risks are and when resources should be applied. In video content creation the diagrams can be myriad – some people draw out sequential workflow tasks, some draw out which assets move through a process, some focus on the people and what they are doing in a workflow, some show the infrastructure or tools that are used at each step. Some visuals include a combination of all the above.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>At MovieLabs we have drawn many of these diagrams, and we’ve exchanged many of them; with our studio partners and others in the industry, and we began to notice one obvious thing – none of the diagrams are the same. Sometimes they vary wildly. Different people, often within the same organization or team, draw different workflow diagrams. Sometimes there are differences within the same diagram with inconsistent approaches to conveying tasks, participants, or assets. While we have no intention of “standardizing” diagrams – because there’s no standard for expressing ideas – we do want to provide a common language that everyone in the industry can use to express their ideas. A language defines concepts and grammar but of course still leaves the user to express their ideas however they want. That, and an underlying data structure across connected ontologies, is what let us to create the MovieLabs Visual Language for Media Creation (VLMC, or Visual Language).</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The Visual Language is intended to be used by those drawing out workflow diagrams, those trying to convey a particular idea or production concept and those creating software or tools to make it easier to create, view and modify those workflows. The Visual Language was intended for Human-to-Human communication, but we also foresee use cases where it can be used for Machine-to-Human Communication (for example a status dashboard showing tasks working through a software defined workflow) or Human-to-Machine Communication (for example drawing out workflows which are then used to program an automated software defined workflow)</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>There are three key components in the Visual Language package:</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>Definition and Guide:</strong> This document describes the various shapes, labels, and icons as well as the lines and arrowheads used to connect shapes in the language. This document also describes the best practices and the connection between the Visual Language and the common Ontology.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>Resources:</strong> This is a set of resources to use the language including icons, shapes, and libraries (with common file formats like SVG and PNG assets).</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>Templates:</strong> The final package is a set of templates in various diagramming or presentation tools. The current templates are in LucidChart, PowerPoint and Visio.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":1} -->
<h1><a id="post-354-_Toc90453896"></a>The Ontology Behind the Visual Language</h1>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The elements depicted in the Visual Language are supported by the MovieLabs Ontology for Media Creation (OMC),<sup><a id="post-354-footnote-ref-2" href="#post-354-footnote-2">[1]</a></sup> which we developed for improving communication, interoperability, and automation of production workflows as part of the MovieLabs 2030 Vision project.<sup><a id="post-354-footnote-ref-3" href="#post-354-footnote-3">[2]</a></sup> The ontology groups workflow elements into general categories including tasks, assets, participants (e.g., organizations, departments, people), contexts (e.g., scenes, shots), infrastructure, and the relationships between them. It also provides some more detailed categories (e.g., image vs. audiovisual assets) and defined terms for many more specific elements. <img class="wp-image-411" src="http://movielabs-api.test/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/word-image-30.png"></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Figure ‑ Ontology for Media Creation</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>In some cases, these categories and defined elements are important enough to have specific depictions in the Visual Language as shapes or icons. In other cases, the names and terms can be used as labels.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Like the use of common shapes and icons, the use of common labels makes it easier to understand what a diagram is trying to illustrate. And the definitions behind the terms give diagrams greater precision. These labels are listed in Appendix A.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>In its initial release, the Ontology covers around 200 of the many thousands of elements in production workflows. When an element in a diagram needs a label, we encourage the use of the common term from the Ontology, if the definition fits and provides the necessary level of specificity. But in many cases, users of the Visual Language will need to create their own names for things. Future releases of the Ontology will expand the coverage still further.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>It’s also worth noting that elements can fall into more than one category. For example, a prop can be regarded as an asset or used as a context for a task. And an automated service can be regarded as a participant performing a task or as infrastructure. So, the shape that is used for an element may vary based on what is being communicated and the message that the creator wants to show. The details of how this works can be found in the ontology documentation.<a id="post-354-_Toc72136514"></a></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The Ontology also defines relationships between elements that can be depicted in the Visual Language, often using lines to connect those elements. For those implementing the Visual Language in software that uses the Ontology, Appendix C describes the visual depiction of relationships in the Ontology.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<h1><a id="post-354-_Toc90453898"></a>Key Workflow Components</h1>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The first order of the Visual Language is to represent the five key categories of workflow components: Assets, Tasks, Participants, Infrastructure and Contexts. We will separately describe the relationships that may connect these in a workflow in the following section “The Meaning of Lines”.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>MovieLabs defines these terms as follows.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul><li><em>Context</em>: Informs scope within the construction process of a Creative Work.</li><li><em>Asset</em>: A physical or digital object or collection of objects specific to the creation of the Creative Work.</li><li><em>Participant</em>: The people, organizations, or services that are responsible for the production of the Creative Work.</li><li><em>Task</em>: A piece of work to be done and completed as a step in the production process.</li><li><em>Infrastructure: </em>The underlying systems and framework required for the production of the Creative Work; it is generally not specific to a particular Creative Work.</li></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>For each of these categories, the Visual Language provides the following:</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul><li><strong>Shape</strong>: A shape that can visually differentiate it from elements in other categories.</li><li><strong>Icons</strong>: Icons that visually identify different classes of elements in the same category. The set of predefined icons will grow over time.</li><li><strong>Labels</strong>: A set of defined terms for classes of elements that can be used to label diagrams consistently.</li></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Users of the Visual Language must use the provided shapes, icons, and labels, unless they need to depict something more specific or otherwise not covered by the current specification.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<h2><a id="post-354-_Toc90453901"></a>Participants</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>Participant</strong>: The people, organizations, or services that are responsible for the production of the Creative Work.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

<h3><a id="post-354-_Toc60162243"></a>Conditional nodes</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>A <em>conditional node</em> is a point in a flow where branching can occur based on the state of the system at the time that the node is reached.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>A <em>forcing factor</em> is an external event that forces one path out of the conditional node to be taken regardless of the state of the system.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Flows may have points where the flow cannot proceed until an event has happened. For this, we use a conditional node where a condition is evaluated, and the flow progresses accordingly. It is not a task itself; it is the assessment of the state after a task or series of tasks. Figure 5‑8 shows some examples.</p>

<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3>Convergence</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>When flows converge it may be necessary to show whether the convergence is blocking or non-blocking.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><em>Blocking Convergence</em> is a point where several flows converge, and flow cannot proceed until all the flows that converge have reached the convergence point.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><em>Non-blocking Convergence</em> is a point where several flows converge, and flow can proceed when any of the flows reach the convergence point.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>To represent convergence, we borrow the “summing junction” and “or” shapes from traditional flowcharts.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":445} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image"><img src="http://movielabs-api.test/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/word-image-53.png" alt="" class="wp-image-445"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Figure ‑ Blocking convergence</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>In the blocking flow, the next activity cannot start until activities X, Y, and Z have completed.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":446} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image"><img src="http://movielabs-api.test/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/word-image-54.png" alt="" class="wp-image-446"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Figure ‑ Non-blocking convergence</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>In the non-blocking flow, the next activity can start as soon as one of activities X, Y, and Z completes.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>What it means for a flow to reach the convergence point is up to the diagram author.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<h2><a id="post-354-_Toc72136521"></a><a id="post-354-_Toc90453908"></a>Messages</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>A <em>Message</em> is a piece of data exchanged between entities such as workflow management elements regarding the control of a workflow.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>For each message, one entity is the sender, and the other entity is the receiver. The Visual Language defines two types of message line. A message is sent from a sender to a receiver and a response (if any) is sent from the receiver to the sender.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The message line is a solid line with an open half arrowhead.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":447} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image"><img src="http://movielabs-api.test/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/shape-description-automatically-generated-with-lo-1.png" alt="Shape

Description automatically generated with low confidence" class="wp-image-447"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Figure ‑ Message arrowhead</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The position of the half arrowhead has meaning.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul><li>The message line has the half arrowhead above a horizontal message line or to the right of a vertical message line</li><li>The response line has the half arrowhead below a horizontal message line or to the left of a vertical message line</li><li>A message line can be double ended and the convention of orientation of the arrows should be followed</li></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<h2><a id="post-354-_Toc67672081"></a><a id="post-354-_Toc72136522"></a><a id="post-354-_Toc90453909"></a>Data movement</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><em>Data movement</em> is the movement of data from one location to another. The movement may be literal, data is copied from one storage location to another, or notional such as the movement of data to a task where the literal movement may be no more than loading the data into system memory.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The data movement line is a solid line with a wide unfilled arrowhead, it indicates the movement of data from one place to another. The arrowhead must be wider than the flow arrow to avoid confusion if the diagramming application does not provide open arrowheads.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":449} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image"><img src="http://movielabs-api.test/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/word-image-56.png" alt="" class="wp-image-449"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Figure ‑ Data movement arrowhead</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>As with flow, a doubled ended data movement line means the same type of data moves in both directions. If a different type of data moves one way than the other, two lines should be used.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":450} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image"><img src="http://movielabs-api.test/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/word-image-57.png" alt="" class="wp-image-450"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Figure ‑ Data movement</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><em>Information movement</em> is the transfer of information that is not data or metadata. For example, the information “the art director says that red lighting won’t work with the costumes in this scene” is sent to the cinematographer or the information “the producer thinks it needs a bake-off around page 30” is sent to the writer. For this purpose, the information movement format is available.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The information input line is a data movement line that is dotted.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<h2><a id="post-354-_Toc72136524"></a><a id="post-354-_Toc90453912"></a>Associations</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>An <em>Association</em> is a way of connecting two or more things in a diagram to show they have a relationship between each other. The word “relationship” is used here in the general sense of that word and not in the formal ontological meaning. Ontological relationships can be expressed using the association construct, however mapping from the ontology to an association is many to one and, therefore, not in the general case reversible.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Associations can be shown using the association line or the grouping shapes.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3><a id="post-354-_Toc72136526"></a>Grouping</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><a id="post-354-_Ref60223723"></a> A group is a boundary drawn around elements in a diagram. It is a shape made up of horizontal and vertical lines with corners that are not radiused. In the simplest case, that is a rectangle, but it can include “T” shapes, “L” shapes and many others. Please note this description of the group construct is not identical to the ontology definition.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Different types of boundary lines are used to represent formal and informal groupings:</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul><li>The formal boundary is a solid line. This construct is used when there is an association between the elements within the boundary. For example, the elements are part of a department, or are part of a workflow.</li><li>The informal or informational boundary is a dashed line. This construct is used when elements are grouped to add clarity or additional information to a diagram.</li></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>A formal boundary has a semantic meaning within the context of the diagram. For example, it groups together the tasks and participants in a department.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>An informal bound is used to group items where the grouping is more general in nature. For example, it groups together elements that are part of post-production.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":457} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image"><img src="http://movielabs-api.test/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/word-image-62.png" alt="" class="wp-image-457"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><a id="post-354-_Ref61615070"></a><a id="post-354-_Ref59192269"></a> Figure ‑ Boundaries</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>In Figure 5‑21, it is important to know which tasks are being performed by which department and who the participants are, and it is useful to know which elements are in principal photography and which are in post-production.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><em>Recommended practice: To distinguish a group from other</em> lines in the diagram, the recommended practice is to use thicker lines of a lighter tone for groups and place the group shape behind all other shapes in the diagram.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>As noted above, there is no requirement for a boundary to be rectangular, but the corners must be right angles (and not radiused), and the lines horizontal or vertical.<sup><a id="post-354-footnote-ref-9" href="#post-354-footnote-9">[8]</a></sup></p>

<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The following is a set of best practices when building diagrams, apps, and dashboards using the Visual Language.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list {"ordered":true} -->
<ol><li><a id="post-354-_Toc90453925"></a> Layout Practices</li></ol>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The recommended practice for laying out diagrams for workflows are:</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul><li>Primary flow is left to right (horizontal) or top to bottom (vertical)</li><li>Diagrams are primarily either horizontal or vertical</li></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Flow can be emphasized by a cascading arrangement flowing left to right.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>To avoid diagrams that are too wide or too tall it may be necessary to “fold” a flow. The recommended practice is that the direction of flow should be maintained. e.g., left to right before and after the fold rather than left to right up to the fold and then right to left beyond the “fold.”</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<h1><a id="post-354-_Toc84425527"></a>Conversion Steps</h1>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The following basic steps are required to convert most MovieLabs Word documents into Markdown. Please ensure you are working with the most recently published/released version of the document, which should have an “RC[#]” in the filename.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list {"ordered":true} -->
<ol><li><strong>Open the document:</strong> In Teams, navigate to the folder containing the Word document that needs to be converted, and open the document in the desktop Word application.</li><li><strong>Save As:</strong> Immediately do a Save As on the document to your working directory (see above). The naming convention is not critical, but it does make it easier if you follow the convention, “[name]-all.”
<ol>

<ul><li>Applies to Word, Excel, PDF, PowerPoint, ZIP files, et al.</li><li>File names should use dashes (“-”) to separate words, and not use underscores, spaces, or other characters.
